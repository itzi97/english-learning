# Fahrenheit 451
**Ray Bradbury**

---

## Thoughts

- People seemed deeply averse to having their worldview challenged when Montag tried to expose them to other ways of seeing the world through reading. What struck me was how *visceral* their reaction was — like antibodies attacking a foreign agent entering the body. It wasn't mere discomfort; it was a violent, instinctive rejection.

- At the end, when Montag is talking to the ex-scholars, Bradbury seems to suggest that we grieve people more if they changed the world — that a life without contribution is less worth mourning. I think that's true on a collective, societal level. But personally, I disagree with it as a rule for how grief actually works: some of the people I've grieved most deeply changed very little of the world in any visible sense. Individual grief doesn't follow that logic. Someone doesn't need to have left a mark on the world to leave one on you.

- The book made me think about what makes someone worth grieving for, and what life looks like without meaning.
